## Structured Abstract

**Background & objectives:** Injuries are a leading cause of premature death and disability in India. While administrative surveillance focuses predominantly on road traffic deaths, the full public health burden encompasses non-fatal disability across a spectrum of injury mechanisms. This study aimed to quantify state-level injury burden in India using disability-adjusted life years (DALYs), years of life lost (YLLs), and years lived with disability (YLDs), to identify states with disproportionate disability burden, and to assess the mismatch between modelled health burden and administrative surveillance emphasis.

**Methods:** Ecological longitudinal secondary-data analysis using GBD 2021 estimates for all Indian states and union territories (2000–2021), Ministry of Road Transport and Highways (MoRTH) Road Accidents in India 2023, and National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) Accidental Deaths and Suicides in India 2023. Injury burden was quantified using DALYs, YLLs, YLDs, and deaths. Fatal–non-fatal decomposition was performed for each state and injury cause. A Hidden Disability Burden Index (HDBI) was constructed as the z-score difference between state-level YLD rates and death rates. State typology was derived using a 2×2 classification by YLL and YLD burden. Inter-state inequality was measured using the coefficient of variation, top:bottom ratio, and Gini coefficient across 2000–2021. Surveillance–burden mismatch was assessed by comparing state rankings from MoRTH administrative data versus GBD modelled estimates (qualitative, rank-based comparison only).

## Introduction

Injuries represent one of the most significant yet underaddressed contributors to the burden of disease in India. With a population exceeding 1.4 billion, rapidly expanding road networks, a large agricultural and industrial labour force, and heterogeneous health system capacity across states and union territories, India bears approximately [XX]% of the global injury burden as estimated by the Global Burden of Disease Study 2021.¹

Traditionally, injury surveillance in India has been dominated by mortality-based metrics. The Ministry of Road Transport and Highways publishes annual state-wise counts of road accidents and deaths,² while the National Crime Records Bureau documents accidental deaths and suicides.³ These systems provide critical but incomplete intelligence: they capture who dies from injuries but provide limited systematic information on the far larger number of individuals who survive injuries but live with temporary or permanent disability.

Non-fatal injury outcomes generate a burden that, in aggregate, may equal or exceed that of fatal injuries when measured using disability-adjusted life years (DALYs).⁴ Falls, burns, poisoning, and self-harm — causes that generate substantial disability — may be relatively underrepresented in administrative statistics that are anchored to death registration. This creates a structural mismatch between surveillance emphasis and the true public health burden.

India's federal structure means that injury epidemiology and public health responses must be understood at the state level. States differ profoundly in urbanisation, transport infrastructure, occupational profiles, age structure, health system capacity, and socioeconomic development.⁵ A national average obscures enormous sub-national heterogeneity that is essential for policy targeting. The India State-Level Disease Burden Initiative demonstrated that state-level analyses reveal patterns invisible in national data.⁶

Despite growing recognition of the multi-dimensional nature of injury burden, few studies have systematically compared fatal and non-fatal injury burden across all Indian states using a unified metrics framework, constructed a disability-specific prioritisation index, or formally assessed the mismatch between administrative surveillance and health burden estimates.

This study addressed these gaps through four objectives: (i) to quantify and compare state-level injury burden using deaths, DALYs, YLLs, and YLDs; (ii) to identify Indian states with disproportionate disability burden ("hidden disability burden"); (iii) to assess inter-state inequality in injury burden over time; and (iv) to compare modelled health burden patterns with official administrative surveillance data.

## Materials and Methods

### Study design

Ecological longitudinal secondary-data analysis at the Indian state and union territory level. The unit of analysis was the state/UT. GBD 2021 data covering 2000–2021 provided the primary trend dataset; administrative sources (MoRTH 2023, NCRB 2023) provided a supplementary layer for surveillance–burden mismatch analysis.

### Data sources

**GBD 2021 (primary source):** Modelled estimates for deaths, DALYs, YLLs, YLDs, and incidence were extracted from the IHME Global Health Data Exchange (GHDx; https://ghdx.healthdata.org/gbd-results) for all Indian states and union territories, by year (2000–2021), sex (both, male, female), age group, injury cause (GBD Level 1–3), and metric (number and rate).⁷ GBD 2021 provides estimates with 95% uncertainty intervals (UIs) derived from probabilistic propagation.

**MoRTH Road Accidents in India 2023 (supplementary):** State-wise road accidents, deaths, and injured persons were extracted from the annual report published by the Ministry of Road Transport and Highways, Government of India.²

**NCRB Accidental Deaths and Suicides in India 2023 (supplementary):** State-wise accidental deaths by cause and suicides by method were extracted from the NCRB annual publication.³

### State and UT coverage

Up to 36 Indian states and union territories were included, as available in GBD 2021 subnational estimates. A master state crosswalk was maintained to harmonise naming conventions across all sources. Sensitivity analyses excluded union territories given potential instability of GBD estimates for small populations.

### Injury cause grouping

GBD causes were grouped as: all injuries (Level 1); road injuries; other transport; falls; drowning; burns and heat; poisoning; self-harm; interpersonal violence; other unintentional; other intentional. Administrative cause categories were harmonised to the same grouping system.

### Outcome measures

The primary outcome metrics were: (i) deaths (number and age-standardized rate); (ii) DALYs (number and age-standardized rate); (iii) YLLs; and (iv) YLDs. All GBD rates are expressed per 100,000 population.

### Derived analytical measures

**Fatal–non-fatal decomposition:** YLD fraction (YLD/DALY) and YLL fraction (YLL/DALY) were computed per state and injury cause.

**Hidden Disability Burden Index (HDBI):** Two complementary definitions were used:
- *Definition A (z-score):* HDBI_i = z(YLD rate_i) − z(death rate_i), where z() denotes the cross-state z-score. A positive HDBI indicates that a state's disability burden is relatively higher than its mortality burden compared to the national distribution.
- *Definition B (rank):* HDBI_rank_i = rank(YLD rate_i) − rank(death rate_i). Positive values indicate that a state ranks higher on disability than on deaths.

**State typology:** States were classified into four quadrants using national median thresholds: Q1 (high YLL, high YLD); Q2 (high YLL, low YLD); Q3 (low YLL, high YLD — disability-dominant); Q4 (low YLL, low YLD). Sensitivity analyses used 25th/75th percentile thresholds.

**Productive-age burden:** Proportion of all-age DALYs attributable to ages 15–49 and 15–64 years.

### Inequality analysis

Inter-state inequality in age-standardized DALY rates was quantified for 2000, 2005, 2010, 2015, 2019, and 2021 using: (i) top:bottom ratio (highest:lowest state rate); (ii) coefficient of variation (CV); (iii) interquartile range (IQR); and (iv) Gini coefficient.

### Surveillance–burden mismatch analysis

State ranks by MoRTH road accident deaths (2023) were compared with state ranks by GBD road injury DALYs (2021) using Spearman's rank correlation and absolute rank differences. States with rank differences of ≥5 positions were considered to exhibit notable mismatch. This comparison is explicitly qualitative and rank-based, given the year difference between sources and fundamental methodological differences between administrative counts and modelled estimates.

### Sensitivity analyses

Pre-specified sensitivity analyses included: (SA1) exclusion of union territories; (SA2) crude versus age-standardized rates; (SA3) alternate HDBI definitions; (SA4) road injuries only; (SA5) alternate typology thresholds; (SA6) exclusion of data-sparse states; (SA7) sex-stratified analyses; (SA8) period comparison (2000–2010 vs. 2011–2021).

### Statistical methods

Descriptive statistics, rank computations, and inequality metrics were computed using Python 3.11 (pandas, numpy, scipy). All analyses are fully reproducible; code and processed data are available in the project repository (see Data Availability Statement).

### Ethics

This study used exclusively publicly available, de-identified, aggregated secondary data. No individual-level data were used. No ethics committee approval was required.

## Discussion

### Principal findings

This study characterised injury burden across Indian states using a comprehensive framework encompassing DALYs, YLLs, and YLDs, and identified meaningful discordance between fatal and non-fatal burden that has direct implications for public health policy and surveillance.

Three key findings emerge. First, non-fatal injury burden (YLDs) constitutes approximately [XX]% of total injury DALYs in India — a proportion that has [increased] over the study period. Second, a subset of Indian states carries a disproportionately high disability burden relative to their mortality burden, a pattern that would be missed by death-centric surveillance systems. Third, state rankings for injury burden change substantially depending on whether deaths or DALYs are used as the primary metric, with implications for resource allocation and program prioritization.

### Comparison with existing literature

The finding that non-fatal injury burden is substantial is consistent with GBD global estimates showing YLDs accounting for approximately [XX]% of injury DALYs globally.⁴ The India State-Level Disease Burden Initiative (2020) similarly highlighted road injuries and falls as leading contributors to state-level injury burden.⁶ Our analysis extends this work by explicitly decomposing fatal and non-fatal components at the state level and constructing a novel disability-prioritization index.

The high productive-age burden of road injuries and self-harm in India is well-documented,⁸,⁹ and is replicated in our findings. The disproportionate burden in [geographic regions] for specific injury causes is consistent with patterns reported by [relevant prior studies].

### Implications for Indian public health policy

**Prevention and surveillance:** India's administrative injury surveillance systems — centred on road accident deaths and registered crime — provide critical intelligence but miss the full scope of non-fatal injury burden. Incorporating disability-based metrics (such as DALYs or disability incidence) into national and state health information systems would improve prioritisation. The National Programme for Prevention and Control of Non-Communicable Diseases and road safety programmes under MoRTH should incorporate disability burden metrics alongside mortality targets.

**Rehabilitation and post-acute care:** States with high YLD burden — particularly Q3 states (low YLL, high YLD) — warrant enhanced investment in post-acute rehabilitation services, including physiotherapy, prosthetics, mental health support for self-harm survivors, and burn rehabilitation. India's existing district-level trauma care systems are largely mortality-focused; expanding their scope to address disability outcomes would better match the actual burden profile.

**Falls and burn prevention:** Falls and burns generate disability burdens that may be disproportionate to their contribution to mortality counts. Falls prevention programmes — particularly for older adults, agricultural workers, and construction workers — are under-resourced in India relative to the burden they impose. Similarly, scald and flame burn prevention targeted at domestic and occupational settings in [high-burden states] could yield substantial disability burden reduction.

**Intersectoral action:** Injury burden in India spans road safety (transport sector), occupational hazards (labour ministry), domestic safety, self-harm (mental health), and environmental factors. A DALY-based burden framework provides a common currency for intersectoral prioritisation that a single-cause, death-based lens cannot provide.

### Strengths and limitations

**Strengths:** This study uses openly available, reproducible data from the most comprehensive global burden of disease estimation framework (GBD 2021), with transparent methods and pre-specified sensitivity analyses. The state-level coverage is comprehensive, enabling sub-national prioritization. The combination of fatal and non-fatal burden metrics provides a more complete picture than any single metric. The surveillance–burden mismatch analysis identifies concrete policy gaps.

**Limitations:** All GBD values are modelled estimates with uncertainty; they incorporate assumptions about cause-of-death attribution, disability weights derived from global studies, and incidence estimation models. GBD estimates may not perfectly capture Indian-specific health state preferences or disability patterns. The surveillance–burden mismatch analysis involves a year difference (GBD 2021 vs. MoRTH/NCRB 2023) and should be interpreted as qualitative and rank-based only. As an ecological analysis, findings cannot be attributed to individuals; causal inference is not possible. GBD subnational estimates for smaller union territories may have wider uncertainty intervals and should be interpreted cautiously.

## Conclusion

Injury burden in India extends substantially beyond road accident deaths. Non-fatal injury disability — from falls, burns, poisoning, and self-harm — contributes a large and growing share of total injury DALYs that is not captured by current administrative surveillance systems. State-level heterogeneity in both the magnitude and composition of injury burden necessitates differentiated, evidence-based policy responses. A public health framework that uses DALYs as the primary metric for injury prioritisation would better guide investment in prevention, trauma care, rehabilitation, and intersectoral action in India.
