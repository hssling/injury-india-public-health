# Plain Language Summary

## What was studied?
This study looked at the full health burden of injuries across all Indian states and union territories — not just deaths, but also disability caused by injuries.

## Why does this matter?
Most official injury data in India focuses on road accident deaths and criminal statistics (accidental deaths and suicides). While these are important, they give an incomplete picture. Many people survive injuries but live with long-term disabilities — from falls, burns, poisoning, and self-harm — that dramatically affect their quality of life and ability to work.

To capture this full burden, we used a measure called Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs), which combines both premature deaths and years lived with disability into a single metric.

## What did we find?
- Injuries in India cause millions of DALYs each year, and a significant share comes from disability, not just death.
- Different states have very different injury burdens — some states have much higher disability from injuries than their death statistics suggest.
- Falls, burns, and self-harm generate substantial disability that is not well captured by current administrative reporting systems.
- Young working-age adults (15–49 years) bear the largest share of injury-related disability.
- There is significant inequality between Indian states in injury burden.

## What are the implications?
Health programs that only count injury deaths are missing a large part of the problem. India's public health planners, state governments, and clinicians should:
- Include disability measures (not just deaths) in injury surveillance
- Prioritize rehabilitation services for injury survivors
- Design targeted prevention programs based on each state's specific injury burden profile

## Who was this study for?
The study findings are most relevant to public health policymakers, state health departments, researchers, and clinicians involved in injury prevention and trauma care in India.
