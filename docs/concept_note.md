# Concept Note
## Burden of Injuries in India: State-Level Fatal–Non-Fatal Decomposition, Inequality, and Surveillance–Burden Mismatch

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-04-23  
**Status:** Draft  

---

## 1. Background and Rationale

Injuries constitute one of the most significant yet under-addressed public health challenges in India. India bears approximately one-fifth of the global injury burden, driven by a combination of rapid motorization, large agricultural and industrial labour force, dense population, and heterogeneous health system capacity across states and union territories.

Despite substantial policy and media attention to road traffic deaths, injury burden in India extends well beyond fatal outcomes. Non-fatal injuries result in disability—temporary and permanent—that imposes enormous economic, social, and care costs on individuals, families, and health systems. Disability-adjusted life years (DALYs), which capture both premature mortality (YLLs) and non-fatal disability (YLDs), provide a more complete picture of this burden than death counts alone.

A critical policy gap exists: Indian surveillance systems (MoRTH road accident reports, NCRB Accidental Deaths and Suicides in India) are primarily death-and-accident focused, while the true burden as measured by DALYs often reflects a very different state priority ordering—particularly for causes like falls, burns, poisoning, and self-harm, which generate substantial disability even when they are not the leading killers.

India is also characterized by extreme heterogeneity across its 28 states and 8 union territories. States differ dramatically in age structure, urbanization, road infrastructure, occupational profiles, health system strength, and socioeconomic development. A national average obscures state-level variation that is essential for policy targeting.

This study addresses these gaps by: (1) quantifying state-level injury burden using the full metric suite (deaths, DALYs, YLLs, YLDs); (2) decomposing fatal versus non-fatal burden; (3) identifying states with "hidden" disability burden; (4) measuring state-level inequality; and (5) mapping mismatches between administrative surveillance emphasis and health-burden-derived priorities.

---

## 2. Research Question

**Primary:** Which Indian states have the greatest injury burden when both premature mortality and disability are considered, and how do state priorities change when burden is assessed using DALYs/YLDs rather than deaths alone?

**Secondary questions:**
- Which states have disproportionately high disability (YLD) burden relative to their mortality (YLL) burden?
- How does cause-specific injury burden vary by state, sex, and age group?
- Has state-level inequality in injury burden increased or decreased between 2000 and 2021?
- Where does official administrative injury surveillance systematically diverge from modeled health burden metrics?
- What are the policy implications of these mismatches for Indian public health?

---

## 3. Study Design

**Type:** Ecological longitudinal secondary-data analysis  
**Unit of analysis:** Indian state/UT  
**Trend period:** 2000–2021 (GBD 2021)  
**Administrative comparison year:** 2023 (MoRTH, NCRB)  
**Data sources:** IHME GBD 2021 (primary modeled estimates); MoRTH Road Accidents in India 2023; NCRB ADSI 2023; India State-Level Disease Burden Initiative (contextual)

---

## 4. Objectives

### Primary Objective
Quantify and compare state-level injury burden in India using deaths, DALYs, YLLs, and YLDs from GBD 2021.

### Secondary Objectives
1. Identify states with high disability-heavy injury burden (hidden disability burden).
2. Compare cause-specific injury burden across states, sex, and age groups.
3. Assess inequality in injury burden across Indian states/UTs.
4. Compare modeled burden patterns with official Indian administrative surveillance data.
5. Generate a public-health prioritization framework for Indian states.

---

## 5. Anticipated Novelty

This study provides novelty through:
- **Decomposition approach:** Separating fatal (YLL) from non-fatal (YLD) burden at state level to reveal hidden disability burden.
- **State-level inequality analysis:** Quantifying and tracking inequality trends in injury burden across Indian states.
- **Surveillance–burden mismatch:** Systematically comparing administrative vs. modeled burden to expose policy blind spots.
- **Multi-dimensional prioritization:** Providing a state-level typology that guides where India should prioritize mortality reduction vs. disability prevention vs. rehabilitation.
- **Indian policy relevance:** Direct implications for India's National Health Policy, Road Safety Policy, and National Programme for Prevention and Control of Non-Communicable Diseases.
